The Returning Citizens Assistance Network (RCAN) is composed of 20 congregations in Washington, DC and works with clients of the Public Defender Service who are returning from a time of incarceration or are still in prison. The network assists these individuals and their families in a variety of different ways with a focus on the practical barriers to effective reentry—housing, clothing, food, transportation, employment and the need for ongoing personal friendship and support.

Since it’s founding in 2018, RCAN has assisted hundreds of individuals and every week we get new referrals of individuals and families who need assistance as they struggle to build stable lives after a time of incarceration. RCAN also provides support and help to those who currently incarcerated through the Prison Friendship Project which matches individuals in the community with men and women who are still in federal prison. These “prison friends” write to the inmate they are matched with and also offer other support during and after a time a time of incarceration. Through these and other programs, the individuals and congregations associated with the Returning Citizens Assistance Network do everything we can to help those who have been in prison find stability and success in their return to the community.

How RCAN Assists (bigger font)
RCAN responds to urgent reentry needs through coordinated support from individuals in partner congregations. Some of the specific ways we help are:

    •	Providing clothing, food, and personal essentials to people who are newly released
    •	Helping to secure housing by paying application fees and security deposits and collecting donated furniture for newly housed individuals
    •	Providing emergency funds to prevent eviction or keep utilities from being disconnected
    •	Offering life-skills counseling, friendship and mentoring relationships
    •	Helping with transportation by providing bicycles that can be used to get around in the community and metro cards that can be used to go to work and medical appointments

Our History

The Returning Citizens Assistance Network traces its origins to 2015 and a presentation that Bryan Stevenson of the Equal Justice Initiative gave at the New York Avene Presbyterian Church (NYAPC) in downtown Washington, DC. During his inspiring talk, Bryan urged the 750 people who were present to “get proximate” to those who are or were incarcerated and become involved in their lives. Shortly after the event, Theo Brown, who had organized the program with Bryan, began to meet with several other members of NYAPC to discuss how they might respond to Bryan’s challenge and help meet the needs of returning citizens and others who are still in prison. As the group began to look into ways they could help, another person who had attended the program with Bryan Stevenson, Rob Hornstein of the DC Public Defender Service, reached out to the Theo with a suggested. He explained that PDS regularly has clients returning from incarceration who have almost no resources and need help as they seek to build a stable life in the community. He proposed that NYAPC work together with PDS to assist their clients and a partnership was born.

At first, there were one or two individuals per month that the group at NYAPC was asked to help and they responded to requests to assist clients who needed furniture for an apartment or help with transportation or emergency food or who were just lonely and needed a friend. After a while, the number of requests began to grow and it became clear that there was much more need than the people at NYAPC could handle. Theo then reached out to Don Craig and other leaders at nearby Metropolitan AME Church and invited them to also help with some of the PDS clients. Several people at Metropolitan AME responded to this need and members of the two churches had conversations about how they might cooperate. After a few months of working together, it was decided to invite other congregations to join a network that would assist PDS clients and, in 2018, NYAPC and Metropolitan AME wrote letters to about 20 congregations inviting them to join the Returning Citizens Assistance Network (RCAN). Aboout a dozen congregations signed up as founding members and RCAN has grown steadily through the years and now has 20 churches and synagogues who work together to assist PDS clients who are returning from incarceration or still in prison.

Our Motivation
The congregations that are part of RCAN are motivated by their spiritual beliefs that each person is a child of God and deserves to be treated with respect and dignity. They are further motivated by commands from their faith traditions that urge followers to show particular concern for the needs of those who are marginalized in our society—the poor, the downtrodden, the despised and those who are in prison.

The Scriptures are filled with the admonition to treat others as we would like to be treated and also to take specific actions that can alleviate the suffering of those who are in physical and mental pain. Some of the many Scriptures that inspire RCAN members are:
• Continue to remember those in prison as if you were together with them in prison, and those who are mistreated as if you yourselves were suffering. (Hebrews 13:3)
• Whoever oppresses the poor shows contempt for their Maker, but whoever is kind to the needy honors God. (Proverbs 14:31)
• And what does the Lord require of you? To act justly and to love mercy and to walk humbly with your God (Micah 6:8)
• Each of you should use whatever gift you have received to serve others, as faithful stewards of God’s grace in its various forms. (1 Peter 4:10)
• Suppose a brother or a sister is without clothes and daily food. If one of you says to them, “Go in peace; keep warm and well fed,” but does nothing about their physical needs, what good is it? (James 2: 14-16)

Our Approach

RCAN is rooted in the conviction that practical support and actions by individuals can reshape reentry outcomes. Congregations in RCAN across Washington, DC share this commitment and, while their traditions are different, they are united in purpose.
• Dignity-first support for people returning to society
• Rapid response to urgent short-term needs
• Congregation-led coordination and care
• Practical pathways toward housing, stability, and work
